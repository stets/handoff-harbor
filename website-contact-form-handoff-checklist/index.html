Forms are not “done” until delivery is proven.

Most client sites have at least one form that matters: quote request, booking, newsletter signup, support request, or checkout-adjacent lead capture. If the form only worked once on your laptop, that is not a handoff.

The handoff should record where submissions go, who owns the form tool, how notifications are sent, what happens after submission, and what the client should do if leads stop arriving.

What to confirm before closing the project

Recipient routing

List every notification address, CRM destination, webhook, Slack alert, autoresponder, and backup inbox tied to each form.

Deliverability proof

Send test submissions from outside the client network, check spam, verify reply-to behavior, and document the test date.

Tool ownership

Confirm who owns forms, SMTP, reCAPTCHA, CRM, newsletter, booking, anti-spam, and paid plugin accounts.

Support boundary

Define whether broken forms, deliverability changes, CRM edits, new fields, spam tuning, and automations are included or billable.

Copy/paste contact form handoff checklist

  • ☐ Every live form URL and purpose is documented.
  • ☐ Notification recipient email addresses are confirmed by the client.
  • ☐ Reply-to address, from address, subject line, and autoresponder behavior are tested.
  • ☐ SMTP/mail service, form plugin, CRM, booking, newsletter, and webhook tools are listed.
  • ☐ reCAPTCHA/turnstile/anti-spam settings are documented.
  • ☐ Test submissions reached the correct inbox, CRM, and backup destination.
  • ☐ Spam folder and domain-authentication issues are noted if relevant.
  • ☐ Client knows where stored submissions live and how long they are retained.
  • ☐ Any forms not covered by support are listed clearly.
  • ☐ Post-launch form edits, new fields, broken notifications, CRM changes, and spam tuning are routed to warranty, care plan, or billable support.
