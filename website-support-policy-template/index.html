Website support policy template for freelance designers

The phrase “support is included” is where unpaid post-launch work sneaks in. A simple support policy tells the client what is covered, what is not, how fast you respond, and when work becomes paid maintenance.

Use this template inside your final handoff packet, project closeout email, or maintenance proposal.

What your post-launch support policy should define

  1. Support window. Name the number of days included after launch or final approval.
  2. Covered issues. Limit included support to bugs related to the approved launch scope.
  3. Excluded work. New pages, new features, content edits, SEO work, training, integrations, and third-party account problems are separate.
  4. Request channel. Tell clients where to send support requests so work does not scatter across text, email, and Slack.
  5. Response time. Give a realistic response window, not an accidental 24/7 promise.
  6. Paid next step. Include the maintenance plan or hourly rate for anything outside the included window.

Copy/paste support policy

Post-launch support policy Your project includes [14/30] days of post-launch bug-fix support after [launch/final approval]. Included support covers bugs or issues related to the approved project scope, such as broken links, layout problems introduced during launch, missing approved assets, or launch-related technical issues. Included support does not cover new pages, new sections, copy changes, new features, SEO campaigns, analytics setup beyond the agreed scope, training, third-party platform changes, domain/email/provider issues, or requests from new stakeholders after approval. Please send support requests to [email/helpdesk/form link]. I respond within [response window] on business days. After the included support window, new requests can be handled through a maintenance plan or quoted separately at $[rate]/hour.

Short version for an email

For support after launch, I include [14/30] days of bug-fix support for issues tied to the approved project scope. New pages, content updates, training, new features, SEO work, third-party account issues, or requests outside the approved scope are separate work. If you want me to keep the site updated, monitored, backed up, and handled after launch, the maintenance option is [maintenance plan/link].

Common mistakes

  • Promising “support” without defining what support means.
  • Letting clients use text messages as the support queue.
  • Covering third-party account or plugin problems for free.
  • Waiting until after the first scope-creep request to mention maintenance.
  • Using harsh legal language when plain boundaries would work better.

Where to put the policy

Put the short version in the final handoff email, the full version in the client handoff packet, and the paid next step in your maintenance plan. That gives the client one clean path instead of a vague “let me know.”
