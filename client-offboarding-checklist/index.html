Client offboarding checklist for web designers

Good offboarding protects your margin, gives the client confidence, and creates the easiest moment to ask for testimonials, referrals, and maintenance work.

Bad offboarding sounds like this: “Here are the login details, let me know if you need anything.” That one sentence can turn a profitable website project into an open-ended support tab.

The client offboarding sequence:
  1. Confirm final approval in writing.
  2. Collect final payment before deep training or open-ended support.
  3. Deliver the handoff packet and access inventory.
  4. Explain what is included after launch.
  5. Explain what becomes paid maintenance or new project work.
  6. Ask for a testimonial while the win is fresh.
  7. Ask for one referral or introduction if the project went well.
  8. Schedule or send a 30-day check-in.

1. Get final approval in writing

Before you offboard, make the finish line explicit. You want a written approval trail that says the current scope is accepted and ready for launch or handoff.

Simple approval language:

“Please reply with ‘approved for launch’ if everything looks good. Once approved, I’ll move into final handoff: documentation, access notes, and post-launch support details.”

2. Tie final payment to the handoff moment

You do not need to be hostile about payment. You do need to avoid creating an accidental incentive where the client gets all deliverables and training before the final invoice is handled.

Keep it calm and procedural: final invoice, then final handoff packet, then support window.

3. Deliver one packet, not five scattered emails

Your offboarding packet should answer the obvious client questions in one place:

  • Where is the live site?
  • Who owns the domain, hosting, CMS, analytics, and email tools?
  • How do we edit common content?
  • What recurring costs should we expect?
  • What do we do if something breaks?
  • What support is included and how do we request more?

4. Ask for the testimonial before the energy fades

The best testimonial ask is specific. Do not ask “can you write me a testimonial?” Ask a short prompt the client can answer quickly.

“If you’re happy with the launch, could you send 2–3 sentences on what changed from the old site to the new one, and what the process felt like? A quick reply is perfect — I can clean up grammar and send it back for approval before using it anywhere.”

5. Offer maintenance as the next safe step

Offboarding does not have to mean disappearing. It should give the client a choice: manage the site themselves with your documentation, or keep you on for support.

Position maintenance around outcomes: backups, updates, small edits, reporting, uptime, speed checks, and someone who already understands the site.

6. Schedule the 30-day follow-up now

Do not rely on memory. Put a 30-day check-in on your calendar or use a canned follow-up script. This is where you catch small issues, ask for the testimonial if you missed it, and convert clients who realized they do not want to manage the site alone.
