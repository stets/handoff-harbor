Website handoff checklist for freelance web designers

A clean website handoff is not just “send the login and hope.” It is a small operating system for QA, access, client confidence, support boundaries, payment, and maintenance.

If launches keep turning into a week of unpaid “quick questions,” use this checklist before sending the final handoff email.

Quick version:
  1. Finish final QA on desktop, tablet, and mobile.
  2. Confirm forms, analytics, automations, and key conversion paths.
  3. Collect account ownership, billing, renewal, plugin, and hosting details.
  4. Prepare one client handoff packet with links, instructions, and known limits.
  5. State what support is included after launch and what becomes paid work.
  6. Send the final invoice/payment note before deep handoff support begins.
  7. Offer an obvious maintenance path for clients who do not want to manage it.

1. Run final QA like the client will click the weird thing first

Check the homepage, core service pages, checkout/contact flows, mobile navigation, footer links, 404 page, and any embedded widgets. Do not stop at “it looks fine on my laptop.”

  • Forms submit to the right inbox or CRM.
  • Thank-you pages and redirects work.
  • Phone/email links work on mobile.
  • Images have sensible alt text and are not gigantic.
  • Meta titles/descriptions are not placeholder copy.
  • 404, privacy, cookie, and legal-ish pages are accounted for where relevant.

2. Put account and access details in one inventory

The client should not have to dig through old emails to figure out who owns the domain, where the site is hosted, or when the plugin renews.

  • Domain registrar and renewal date.
  • Hosting/platform account owner.
  • CMS admin URL and role assignments.
  • Analytics/Search Console access.
  • Paid plugins, apps, integrations, and renewal ownership.
  • DNS/email dependencies.

Never put raw passwords in a PDF. Use a password manager, secure sharing, or the client’s own account invite flow.

3. Write the client handoff packet

The packet is the “where everything is” document. It should be boring, direct, and useful.

  • Final site URL and staging URL, if still relevant.
  • What was delivered.
  • How to make common edits.
  • What not to touch without help.
  • Important recurring costs.
  • Support window and response expectations.

4. Make support boundaries explicit

Most post-launch resentment comes from fuzzy boundaries. Say what is included, what is excluded, and how new work is handled.

Example language:

“Your project includes 14 days of bug-fix support for issues related to the approved launch scope. New pages, copy changes, feature requests, third-party tool issues, or training beyond the handoff notes can be handled as a separate support request or maintenance plan.”

5. Attach the maintenance offer while the value is obvious

The best time to offer maintenance is when the client is happy the site is live and realizes there are still updates, plugins, backups, reporting, and small edits to manage.

  • Keep tiers simple.
  • Explain the risk removed, not just the hours included.
  • Give a clear “reply yes and I’ll set it up” next step.
