Web design client handoff template

A client handoff template should make the finish line boring: what was delivered, where everything lives, who owns what, what support is included, and what becomes paid maintenance.

If your handoff is a pile of links in an email thread, the client will keep asking the same questions after launch. Use this structure instead.

Copy this handoff packet structure:
  1. Final project summary and approval status.
  2. Live website URL, staging URL, and admin/editing links.
  3. Delivered pages, templates, assets, integrations, and known exclusions.
  4. Account ownership and access inventory.
  5. Common editing instructions and “do not touch this” notes.
  6. Support window, bug-fix scope, and response expectations.
  7. Maintenance plan option for updates, backups, reporting, and small edits.

Final project summary

Start with the simple version of what changed: the project goal, what went live, and what approval means. This avoids the client treating handoff as another open revision phase.

Links and access

Include the live site, admin URL, analytics, Search Console, hosting, domain registrar, CRM/forms, email tools, and any third-party services. Do not paste passwords into the packet; document where access is managed and invite the right client users.

Editing instructions

Do not write a full CMS manual. Write the five edits this specific client will actually make: text, images, blog posts, team members, service areas, products, or landing-page blocks.

Support boundaries

Spell out the difference between bugs in the approved launch scope and new work. This one section can save hours of unpaid “quick fix” work.

“Your project includes 14 days of bug-fix support for issues related to the approved scope. New pages, copy changes, feature requests, training, or third-party tool issues can be handled as a separate support request or maintenance plan.”

Maintenance offer

Give clients a next step if they do not want to manage the site alone. Keep it simple: updates, backups, small edits, reporting, uptime checks, and priority support.
