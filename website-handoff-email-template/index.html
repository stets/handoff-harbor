Website handoff email template

Your final website handoff email should not be a celebratory link dump. It should close the project, document access, define support, and give the client a clear next step.

Use this structure when a client site is live and you need to hand it over without creating two weeks of unpaid cleanup work.

What the handoff email needs to do

  • Confirm the live site and final approval status.
  • Point the client to one handoff packet instead of scattered links.
  • Explain where account access, renewals, and ownership notes live.
  • State what is included in the post-launch support window.
  • Separate bugs from new work, training, and content edits.
  • Tie final payment and maintenance options to the handoff.

Copy/paste handoff email

Subject: Your website is live — handoff packet + next steps Hi [Client Name], Your new website is live here: [Live URL] I put the handoff details in one packet so everything is easy to find later: [Link to handoff packet] Inside you’ll find: - live site and admin/editing links - delivered pages and approved scope - account ownership and renewal notes - basic editing instructions - analytics/form/SEO setup notes - post-launch support details - recommended maintenance next steps For the next [14/30] days, I’ll handle bug fixes related to the approved launch scope. New pages, new features, copy changes, training, third-party tool issues, or requests outside the approved scope can be handled as a separate support request or monthly maintenance plan. The final invoice is here: [Invoice link] If you want help keeping the site updated, backed up, and monitored, the maintenance option I recommend is: [Short maintenance offer] Thanks again — this was a fun one to bring live. [Your Name]

Payment-first variation

If your contract says final files or ownership transfer happens after final payment, do not be coy. Keep the tone friendly, but make the sequence obvious.

Subject: Final website handoff — invoice + access packet Hi [Client Name], The site is ready for final handoff. Once the final invoice is paid, I’ll send the complete handoff packet with admin links, access notes, delivered assets, support-window details, and maintenance options. Final invoice: [Invoice link] After payment clears, I’ll send the packet and confirm the live-site access details in one place so nothing gets buried in email.

Common mistakes

  • Sending passwords in plain email. Invite users or use a password manager instead.
  • Forgetting support boundaries. “Let me know if you need anything” sounds nice, but it creates scope fog.
  • Waiting too long to offer maintenance. The best time to offer support is when the client is excited and the system is fresh.
  • Scattering links across multiple emails. One packet beats a thread full of random URLs.
