Webflow handoff gets expensive when client ownership is fuzzy.

Webflow projects can hide a lot of loose ends: who owns the workspace, who pays the site plan, where form notifications go, who can edit CMS items, which integrations use whose API keys, and what happens when the client wants a new section after launch.

This is not legal, accounting, or platform policy advice. It is a practical closeout checklist for freelance designers and small studios so clients know what they own, what they can edit safely, and what becomes paid support after launch.

What to document before a Webflow project is accepted

Workspace, billing, and roles

Confirm the client owns the Webflow workspace or site, site plan, billing email, collaborators, editor seats, recovery email, and 2FA expectations.

Domains, DNS, and launch settings

Record custom domains, DNS records, SSL status, default domain, redirects, locale settings, backups, staging link, and publish workflow.

CMS, forms, and integrations

List CMS collections, required fields, form destinations, spam handling, Zapier/Make automations, newsletter/CRM routes, API keys, embeds, and custom code.

Editing and support boundaries

Separate client-safe edits from paid changes: new sections, complex CMS changes, layout tweaks, SEO work, integrations, forms, ecommerce, and troubleshooting.

Copy/paste Webflow handoff checklist

  • ☐ Client controls the correct Webflow workspace/site ownership, billing email, and site plan renewal.
  • ☐ Collaborator, editor, agency, and contractor access has been reviewed and unnecessary access removed.
  • ☐ Custom domains, DNS records, SSL status, default domain, and registrar ownership are documented.
  • ☐ Publishing flow is clear: production domain, staging link, backup/snapshot expectations, and who can publish.
  • ☐ Forms are tested; notification recipients, storage, spam protection, and CRM/newsletter destinations are documented.
  • ☐ CMS collections, required fields, image sizes, slug rules, and client editing limits are explained.
  • ☐ SEO settings, redirects, sitemap, robots, analytics, Search Console, pixels, and conversion checks are transferred or documented.
  • ☐ Custom code, third-party embeds, APIs, automation tools, payment/booking widgets, and renewal costs are listed.
  • ☐ Ecommerce, memberships, localization, and paid add-ons are tested or explicitly marked not in scope.
  • ☐ Known issues, accepted tradeoffs, future recommendations, and client signoff are recorded.
  • ☐ Included launch support window is stated, and new CMS, design, SEO, integration, and form work routes to paid support.

Short Webflow handoff email

Subject: Webflow site handoff + support boundaries Hey [Name] — the Webflow site is ready for final handoff. Before we call the project accepted, I want to make sure the operating pieces are clear: workspace/site ownership, billing, domains/DNS, forms, CMS editing notes, integrations, analytics, backups/publishing, and the support window. Please review the handoff notes below by [date]. Launch bugs tied to the original scope are covered through [support end date]. New sections, CMS restructuring, SEO work, integration changes, form routing, ecommerce changes, or ongoing maintenance can be handled through a separate change request or care plan.
